Council supports proposed Nisga’a health clinic in Terrace

Published 7:44 am Tuesday, November 18, 2025

By Jake Wray

The Nisga’a Valley Health Authority is seeking funding to establish a fixed-location outreach clinic in Terrace to augment its mobile outreach clinic van, seen here. (Pacific Northwest Division of Family Practice photo)

Jake Wray

Terrace city council has thrown its support behind a grant application to establish a permanent, stationary medical outreach clinic in Terrace, though the move sparked debate over where such a facility might be located.

At its Nov. 10 meeting, council reviewed a request from Raina Trappl, medical outreach clinic manager for the Nisga’a Valley Health Authority.

NVHA is applying for funding through Health Canada’s Emergency Treatment Fund to establish outreach clinic services at a fixed storefront location.

According to the request, the new stationary clinic is being developed in partnership with the Coast Mountain Primary Care Network. It aims to provide low-barrier, multidisciplinary care to the community’s most vulnerable — specifically those facing substance use challenges, mental health concerns, and housing instability.

“Building on our previous outreach work with the medical outreach clinic van… our primary care services will include opioid agonist treatment, wound care, cancer screening, harm reduction supports, and culturally safe system navigation,” Trappl wrote in her request to council.

While the request for a letter of support was ultimately successful, it drew scrutiny from Councillor James Cordeiro.

He raised concerns about the shift from a dispersed mobile model to a fixed location, noting the potential for unintended consequences in the surrounding neighbourhood.

“The reality is they do tend to have a negative impact on the immediate proximity to the facility,” Cordeiro argued. He questioned if a proposed location had been identified, to which staff replied it had not.

Cordeiro cautioned that while the services are needed, a fixed low-barrier site can “concentrate” social issues.

“The service provider doesn’t really have a responsibility after [clients] leave the area… but there is an impact that is created by concentrating those services,” he said.

Councillor Brian Downie pushed back against the concerns, citing the success of the current mobile unit. “I see none of the examples that you’re referring to,” Downie said, praising the doctor, nurse, and social worker currently delivering care on the street. “It’s just a very effective service for people that will not go into an institution.”

Mayor Sean Bujtas acknowledged Cordeiro’s points regarding neighbourhood impacts but argued that securing the funding and services should be the priority. He suggested that zoning and location issues could be addressed if and when the project moves forward.

“These are things that we really need,” Bujtas said. “I don’t believe that this is going to have a negative impact, but there’s always that concern.”

Council voted to provide the letter of support for the grant application to Health Canada.
